\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Motivation}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) are necessary to protect computers from malicious network traffic. \textbf{Data poisoning} has the potential to corrupt the model by altering its training dataset.
		\item The defense must run \textbf{before training}: unsupervised, with no trustworthy labels.
		\item Monkam et al.\ [1] filter poison topologically --- bytes $\to$ image $\to$ cubical persistence $\to$ clustering. \textbf{What can that lens see?}
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Related Work}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Cohen-Steiner et al.\ (2005) [3].} Diagrams are stable: small perturbations give small bottleneck change. The warrant for topology as a detector.
		\item \textbf{Monkam, De Lucia \& Bastian (2024) [1].} The pipeline reconstructed here; reports $40$--$70\%$ of poison removed.
		\item \textbf{Ferrara (2025) [2].} Thm.\ 2 couples classification error and topological distortion --- under hypotheses (linear classifiers, Vietoris--Rips, Frobenius balls) that all fail here. A complementary axis: \emph{which points} to attack, where we ask \emph{which algebra} registers.
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Research Question}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Hypothesis:} our low capture rate came from unrealistically weak synthetic poison. \textbf{Test:} a surrogate-guided, validity-preserving swap attack.
		\item \textbf{Refuted.} Nothing approached $40$--$70\%$; the highest capture under any committed configuration was $6.48\%$. The gap is structural, not a matter of realism.
	\end{itemize}

	\innerblock{}{
		\centering
		\textbf{What determines topological detectability --- the attack's invariance structure, or the filtration's sensitivities?}
	}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Methods I: An Invariance Argument}{
	\raggedright
	A swap attack applies $\sigma \in S_{1500}$ to the payload $x \in \{0,\dots,255\}^{1500}$. Binarization $b$ is pointwise, so it commutes with $\sigma$:

	\innerblock[bodywidth=0.86\linewidth]{}{
	\[
		\underbrace{b(x \circ \sigma) = b(x) \circ \sigma}_{\text{equivariance}}
		\ \ \Longrightarrow\ \
		\underbrace{\sum_{i} b\big((x \circ \sigma)_i\big) = \sum_{i} b(x_i)}_{\text{count is invariant}}
	\]
	}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Equivariance is not invariance.} Binarization commutes; the \emph{count} is what is fixed. Both steps are needed, and the result holds for every $\sigma$, not just the ${\sim}60$ transpositions applied.
		\item Every count-derived feature is therefore blind to permutation. Detection must flow through the \textbf{position-dependent} filtrations (Height, Radial). Invariance that is too coarse is a known hazard --- the motivation for group-equivariant operators [4].
		\item The threshold is a fraction of the batch maximum --- data-dependent, but a maximum is itself permutation-invariant.
	\end{itemize}

	\begin{center}
	\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/figure_v2_binarized_triptych}
	\end{center}

	\innerblock{}{
		\centering
		\textbf{The count channel is provably insensitive to permutation; the position channel stays open.}\\
		Attenuated on the foreground-count channel --- not absent.
	}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Methods II: Design}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item UNSW-NB15, $5{,}000$ packets, $10\%$ poison, $5$ seeds. Each $1500$-byte payload $\to$ $30 \times 50$ image; $5$ filtrations $\times$ $6$ vectorizations $\times$ $2$ homology dimensions $= 60$ features --- the binarize--filtration--cubical pattern of Garin \& Tauzin [5]. OPTICS reported; other clusterers $\approx 0$.
		\item \textbf{Capture} $=$ poison landing in \emph{$100\%$-pure} clusters. Zero-tolerance: a $99\%$-poison cluster counts for nothing.
		\item Four multiset-preserving families, so every poisoned packet stays well-formed. Invariance measured directly: $1{,}600$ count checks ($200$ packets $\times$ $4$ families $\times$ $2$ thresholds), Gaussian noise as positive control.
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Results: Four Families, One Proof}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{l|c|c|c}
		\textbf{Family} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{Median} & \textbf{Capture}\\
		 & \textbf{change} & \textbf{bytes moved} & \textbf{(\%)}\\
		\hline
		Block reversal ($k{=}120$) & $0/200$ & $0$ & $\mathbf{0.00 \pm 0.00}$\\
		Block swap ($2 \times 60$) & $0/200$ & $0$ & $\mathbf{0.00 \pm 0.00}$\\
		Transpositions ($60$) & $0/200$ & $8$ & $1.80 \pm 0.51$\\
		Cyclic shift & $0/200$ & $89$ & $6.28 \pm 1.31$\\
	\end{tabular}
	\end{center}

	\begin{center}
	\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/figure_v3_four_families}
	\end{center}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Identical invariance, a six-point spread in capture.} The invariance class alone does not determine detectability.
		\item \textbf{The silent families barely move anything:} median $0$ bytes changed; after binarization $88\%$ and $83\%$ of packets are \emph{bit-identical} to their originals, giving identical feature vectors.
		\item \textbf{Random swaps are quiet for a computable reason:} foreground fraction $p \approx 0.042$, so only $2p(1-p) \approx 8\%$ of transpositions change the image. Control: noise shifts the count by $|\Delta| = 14.02$.
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Discussion}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Capture tracks spatial disruption, not sophistication:} unguided cyclic shift ($6.28\%$) matches surrogate-guided swaps ($6.48\%$).
		\item \textbf{A reported negative result.} Guidance gave $+4.68 \pm 1.57$ ($5/5$ seeds) on UNSW-NB15 but \textbf{does not replicate} on CICIDS2017: $-0.60 \pm 3.03$, $2/5$ seeds, per-seed $[-0.4, -2.2, +0.4, +4.2, -5.0]$ --- the sign flips and the mean crosses zero. Pre-committed; not rescued.
		\item The invariance is elementary folklore; we find no prior statement of it for this pipeline. Scope: \emph{multiset-preserving} perturbations. Whether \emph{functional} reorderings in the same group --- header order, padding placement --- inherit the attenuation is a conjecture this motivates, \textbf{not a result}.
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Conclusions}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item One channel of the detector is \textbf{provably insensitive} to permutation attacks. No amount of data can change that.
		\item \textbf{Insensitive is not undetectable:} four identically invariant families span $0\%$--$6.3\%$.
		\item Detectability is a property of the \textbf{(attack, detector) pair} --- a detector's blind spots can be read off the algebra of its feature map before any experiment.
	\end{itemize}

	\innerblock{}{
		\small
		\textbf{Limitations.} Proof: any fixed-threshold pipeline. Empirics: one reconstruction, one primary dataset, two thresholds, $5$ seeds; cross-dataset replication attempted and failed.
	}
}
