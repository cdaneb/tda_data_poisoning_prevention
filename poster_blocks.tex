\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Motivation}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item ML-based intrusion detection trains on traffic adversaries partly control; \textbf{data poisoning} injects corrupted samples to degrade accuracy.
		\item Defense must act \textbf{before training} --- no trained model, no trustworthy labels.
		\item Monkam et al.\ (2024) apply \textbf{topological data analysis} here. \textbf{What can that lens see?}
	\end{itemize}
}


\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Related Work}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Cohen-Steiner et al.\ (SoCG~'05, Pisa, pp.~263--271).} Stability: small data perturbations give small topological change --- this licenses topology as a detector.
		\item \textbf{Monkam, De Lucia \& Bastian (2024).} Payload bytes $\to$ images $\to$ cubical persistence $\to$ clustering removes poisoned packets pre-training.
		\item \textbf{Ferrara (2025).} A \emph{complementary axis} --- which \emph{points} an attack should target, where we ask which \emph{perturbation algebra} the detector registers. Thm.\ 2 assumes linear classifiers, Vietoris--Rips, Frobenius-ball perturbations; none hold here, so it is not under test.
	\end{itemize}
}


\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Research Question}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Hypothesis:} our low detection rate was caused by unrealistically weak synthetic poison.
		\item \textbf{Test:} a realistic attack --- surrogate NIDS classifier $+$ genetic search over multiset-preserving byte swaps.
		\item \textbf{Result:} detectability is \emph{not} ordered by realism.
	\end{itemize}

	\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{l|c}
		\textbf{Attack} & \textbf{Poison captured}\\
		\hline
		Random swaps        & $1.80\% \pm 0.51$\\
		Noise $+$ swaps     & $3.52\% \pm 1.79$\\
		Gaussian noise      & $4.96\% \pm 1.64$\\
		\textbf{Guided swaps} & $\mathbf{6.48\% \pm 1.24}$\\
	\end{tabular}
	\end{center}

	\innerblock{}{
		\centering
		\textbf{What determines whether poisoning is topologically detectable --- the attack's invariance structure, or the filtration's sensitivities?}
	}
}


\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Methods I: An Invariance Argument}{
	\raggedright
	A swap attack permutes byte positions: $\sigma \in S_{1500}$ acting on $x \in \{0,\dots,255\}^{1500}$. Binarization $b$ is \emph{pointwise}, so it commutes with $\sigma$:

	\innerblock[bodywidth=0.8\linewidth]{}{
	\[
		\underbrace{b(x \circ \sigma) = b(x) \circ \sigma}_{b \ \text{is \emph{equivariant}}}
		\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
		\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{i} b(x_i) \ \text{unchanged}}_{\text{the \emph{count} is \emph{invariant}}}
	\]
	}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Permutations \textbf{rearrange} foreground pixels --- never change how many. Only \textbf{position-dependent} filtrations (Height, Radial) can respond.
		\item Noise changes byte \emph{values}, crosses the threshold, and registers in \textbf{every} channel.
		\item \textbf{Confirmed:} permutation changed the count in $0$ of $200$ samples, all four families; noise shifted it by $|\Delta| = 14.02$.
		\item \textbf{Scope:} \texttt{Binarizer(0.4)} cuts at a fraction of the \emph{fitted} \texttt{max\_value\_}, so the threshold is data-dependent. The argument survives because a maximum is itself permutation-invariant --- verified in code, not assumed.
	\end{itemize}

	\begin{center}
	\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/figure_v2_binarized_triptych.pdf}
	\end{center}

	\innerblock{}{
		\centering
		\textbf{Attenuated on the foreground-count channel --- not absent.} That channel is \emph{provably insensitive} to permutation; the position channel stays open. Elementary mathematics --- but we find no prior statement of it for this pipeline.
	}
}


\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Methods II: Design}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{itemize}
		\item UNSW-NB15, $5{,}000$ samples, $10\%$ poison, \textbf{5 seeds}; $60$ topological features; OPTICS reported (other algorithms $\approx 0$).
		\item A \textbf{surrogate classifier} was required --- the cited attacks are model-relative, but the source paper specifies none. MLP ($0.969$), RF cross-check ($0.985$). These are \emph{attacker-side} accuracies; capture \% below is \emph{detector-side}.
	\end{itemize}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Results}{
	\raggedright
	\begin{center}
	\begin{tabular}{l|c|c}
		\textbf{Permutation family} & \textbf{Mean pos.\ changed} & \textbf{Poison captured}\\
		\hline
		Block reversal ($k=120$)    & $14.08$  & $\mathbf{0.00\% \pm 0.00}$\\
		Block swap ($2 \times k=60$) & $17.63$  & $\mathbf{0.00\% \pm 0.00}$\\
		Transpositions ($60$)       & $22.93$  & $1.80\% \pm 0.51$\\
		Cyclic shift                & $281.09$ & $6.28\% \pm 1.31$\\
	\end{tabular}
	\end{center}

	\begin{center}
	\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/figure_v3_four_families.pdf}
	\end{center}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Detectability tracks spatial disruption, not realism.} Unguided cyclic shift ($6.28\% \pm 1.31$) matches surrogate-guided search ($6.48\% \pm 1.24$): guidance is an efficient route to disruption, not a distinct phenomenon.
		\item \textbf{Guidance --- UNSW-specific.} $+4.68 \pm 1.57$, 5/5 seeds on UNSW-NB15. \textbf{CICIDS2017: $-0.60 \pm 3.03$, 2/5 seeds positive}, per-seed $\Delta$ $[-0.40, -2.20, +0.40, +4.20, -5.00]$. Sign flips per seed; the mean crosses zero. Reported as a genuine negative under pre-commitment.
		\item \textbf{No configuration} approached the $40$--$70\%$ reported by the source paper; the highest capture under any committed configuration is $6.48\%$.
	\end{itemize}
}

\block[titleleft,roundedcorners=16]{Why Permutations?}{
	\raggedright
	Permutations are the \textbf{probe}, not the proposed attack: $S_{1500}$ is precisely the group under which the foreground count is invariant, so it isolates exactly what that channel cannot see. The concerning case is the \emph{functional} reordering living in the same group --- HTTP header order, chunked-segment order, padding placement. Whether such attacks inherit the attenuation is a \textbf{conjecture this work motivates, not a result}.
}
